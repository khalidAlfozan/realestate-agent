## 1. Property summary

This is a 73 m², four-room flat occupying the ground floor of a four-storey, 1959-built brick block at ul. Marcina Kasprzaka 9 in the Czyste sub-district of Wola. The layout is fully separated (four independent, non-pass-through rooms plus a separate kitchen), ceiling height is 2.80 m, and the unit includes a loggia of approx. 6 m² (excluded from the quoted surface) and a cellar. The property is held under **limited (spółdzielcze własnościowe) ownership** — a co-op right rather than full freehold — which places restrictions on mortgage access and resale speed. Heating is **urban (district) heating**, which is cost-predictable and tenant-friendly. The seller states the property has been vacant since a full architect-supervised renovation and is available from 4 May 2026; the listing notes it is currently reserved, suggesting an earlier transaction may have fallen through or is pending.

## 2. Neighbourhood context

**District character.** Wola is Warsaw's most rapidly transforming inner-ring district, hosting the city's emerging second CBD (Rondo Daszyńskiego/Towarowa axis) alongside established residential streets. The Czyste sub-district specifically sits between the older residential fabric of western Wola and the new-build towers cluster near Rondo Daszyńskiego — a mixed micro-location with good functional infrastructure (schools, medical facilities, retail) without the premium address of Browary Warszawskie or Mennica Residence. The target tenant for this property is most likely a family with children (four independent rooms, ground-floor loggia, adjacent playground, nearby primary school) or a group of working adults sharing costs, rather than the single-professional market that gravitates toward modern studio/2-room stock.

**Demographics.** Wola holds approximately 150,200 residents across 19 km² — a density of roughly 7,905 per km² (GUS BDL 2024/2025) — making it one of Warsaw's denser districts. Net migration was +408 in 2024 (GUS BDL), a modest but positive demand tailwind confirming the district is still attracting more residents than it loses. However, 7.2 new dwellings completed per 1,000 residents in 2025 (GUS BDL) is a meaningful supply-pressure signal: Wola is adding new stock at a pace that keeps tenant choice high and limits rent growth, particularly for older secondary-market properties competing against freshly delivered new-builds. Commercial vitality is very high at 409 businesses per 1,000 residents (GUS BDL 2024), reflecting the district's dual identity as a residential and employment hub — a structural positive for the tenant pool.

**Amenities.** Transit access is excellent. The nearest subway infrastructure is 523 m away (consistent with the listing's stated 550 m to metro), placing the property within a comfortable walking distance of the M2 line at Rondo Daszyńskiego — the most rent-relevant single transit signal in Warsaw. Within the 800 m walkability radius there are 23 tram stops (nearest: Szpital Wolski 07 at 332 m and Karolkowa 04 at 337 m) and 59 bus stops (nearest: Szpital Wolski 01 at just 264 m, with Rondo Daszyńskiego stops at 424 m), giving access to 13 bus lines and 4 tram lines as the seller notes. Schools are strong: Szkoła Podstawowa Nr 387 im. Szarych Szeregów is 224 m away (within the wider 1,200 m school catchment radius), and there is a liceum at 287 m — a genuine positive for a family tenant profile. Green space is limited at this density: the nearest park-equivalent is Skwer Alojzego Pawełka at 549 m and Park Wolności at 613 m, adequate but not a standout.

## 3. Market backdrop

Warsaw's secondary market has been in a pronounced nominal price-growth phase: NBP's 2024 Q1 housing-market report records transaction prices for 60–80 m² dwellings in Warsaw rising 24.1% year-on-year nominally in Q1 2024, with offer prices deviating from transactions by approximately 2.3% — a notable narrowing of the bid-ask gap that suggests sellers have had pricing power. Real (wage-deflated) price growth was a more modest 10.2% y/y in the same period (NBP 2024 Q1), reflecting that wage growth has partially absorbed the nominal appreciation. The secondary market at Kasprzaka-style price points (15,000–19,000 PLN/m²) therefore sits in a segment that has appreciated but less explosively than the ultra-premium new-build layer — the district comp set shows exactly this two-tier structure.

The rental side is more cautious. NBP's 2024 Q1 report explicitly notes that "the level of average, transactional rental rates per sq. m of housing decreased (q/q) in all groups of cities," attributing this to a wave of newly completed dwellings that were purchased during the investment boom of 2020–2023 now hitting the rental market simultaneously (NBP 2024 Q1). The 2023 Q4 report reinforces this: "In 2023, the number of dwellings offered for rent was on the rise… a result of the completion of dwellings purchased in the boom period of 2020–2022, when many investors decided to purchase rental housing" (NBP 2023 Q4). In Wola this is highly visible — Towarowa Towers, Browary Warszawskie, Mennica Residence, and several other projects have delivered or are delivering thousands of units at the premium end, structurally pressuring achievable rents for older secondary-market stock competing for the same tenant pool. The GUS 7.2 new-dwellings rate for Wola 2025 confirms supply pressure remains live.

Structurally, the rental market's demand base in Warsaw remains solid — driven by employment concentration, student population, and continued inward migration of workers from other Polish cities and abroad — but the NBP analysis suggests the market is moving toward supply-demand equilibrium in large cities after the post-pandemic supply crunch. For a long-term rental investor, this means rent growth at the older-stock level is likely to be modest in real terms over the next two to three years, and vacancy management will matter more than it did in 2021–2022.

## 4. Condition assessment

The seller claims a full architect-supervised renovation (new floors, gypsum walls, electrical installation, lighting, radiators, kitchen, bathroom, doors) with no occupancy since completion. Photo-derived condition: **EXCELLENT (high confidence, 17 photos analysed)**. The photos corroborate every seller claim without contradiction: engineered-wood flooring uniformly installed throughout, smooth white gypsum walls without cracks or moisture marks in any room, a completely new kitchen with integrated stainless-steel appliances and contemporary finishes, a fully tiled bathroom with walk-in glass shower and heated towel rail, new vertical and panel radiators, modern track and recessed lighting, and factory-fresh interior doors. Notably, large windows face what appears to be green space, and no signs of damp, settlement, or wear are visible anywhere. The finish quality is consistent with professional, supervised work and not a superficial cosmetic refresh. The only caveat applicable to any 1959-era ground-floor flat regardless of renovation quality is subsurface moisture risk: no current evidence exists in the photos, but this is an inherent structural characteristic that should be verified with a technical inspection before exchange of contracts.

- **Asking price:** 1 290 000 PLN (= 17 671 PLN/m² vs sale comp median 21 800 PLN/m² → **19% discount**, fully structurally explained).
- **Monthly community fee (czynsz):** 930 PLN (≈ 12.7 PLN/m²) — within the normal 8–15 PLN/m² range for a 1959-era block with district heating; not a red flag but at the higher end of typical.
- **Estimated monthly rent:** 5 770 PLN.
- **Annual rent:** 69 240 PLN.
- **Gross yield: 5.37%** (from `calculate_gross_yield`) — technically within the typical Warsaw 5–7% range, but at the low end of it.
- **Net rent after czynsz:** 5 770 − 930 = **4 840 PLN/month**; **net yield ≈ 4.50%** (4 840 × 12 / 1 290 000) — below the 5% floor of the typical gross-yield range, and before any management fees, vacancy, or capex.
- The gross yield clears 5% marginally; the net yield does not. A single month of vacancy per year would reduce effective net yield to approximately 4.1%, and a 7% property management fee would shave another 40 basis points. The investment's cash economics are thin at the asking price.

## 7. Risks and sensitivities

- **Limited (co-op) ownership — structural liquidity risk.** Spółdzielcze własnościowe prawo do lokalu means bank financing for a buyer on exit is more restricted (some lenders decline or charge higher margins), the buyer pool at resale is narrower, and transactions take longer. This is the most structurally significant risk and the principal reason for the price discount. It does not affect rental income but materially affects exit optionality and holds-to-liquidity.
- **Ground floor in a 1959 block — moisture and security risk.** No moisture evidence is visible in the current photos, but the renovation has been completed recently; subsurface moisture in older pre-fab or brick-block ground floors may take 12–24 months of heating cycles to manifest. A professional structural/damp survey before purchase is strongly recommended. Ground-floor position also reduces tenant pool depth (some tenant segments decline ground-floor offers on security grounds) and supports a 5–10% rent discount vs upper floors.
- **Supply pressure from Wola new-build completions.** GUS BDL records 7.2 new dwellings completed per 1,000 Wola residents in 2025, and NBP's 2024 Q1 report explicitly notes that rental rates fell q/q in all large cities as boom-era investor purchases enter the rental market. A renovated 1959 block competes directly with premium modern stock for the higher-paying segment of the tenant market and will be structurally disadvantaged in those comparisons over the medium term.
- **Net yield sensitivity to vacancy and costs.** At the current asking price, one month's vacancy per year plus a 7% management fee reduces effective net yield to approximately 3.6–3.8% — a level that does not adequately compensate for the illiquidity inherent in this ownership form and property profile.
- **Asking-price discount is structural, not opportunistic.** The 19% discount to the sale comp median is real but is almost entirely accounted for by ground floor, 1959 build year, and limited ownership. There is no identifiable "mispricing" to capture; the discount is baked into the asset's intrinsic characteristics. Any investor should not expect price-convergence toward the comp median over time — these structural features do not disappear.

## 8. Recommendation

**Verdict: Borderline.**
**Confidence: Medium.**

The property has genuine merit: independently verified excellent condition (architect renovation, never occupied), outstanding transit access (Metro line 2 at 550 m, tram at 332 m, bus at 264 m), a differentiated four-independent-room layout well-suited to families, a structurally honest asking-price discount, and urban heating that reduces tenant cost friction. However, the economics are too thin at 1 290 000 PLN to rate this a clear Buy. Gross yield of 5.37% sits at the bottom of the acceptable range; net yield after the 930 PLN monthly czynsz is 4.50% before management or vacancy; and three structural constraints — limited co-op ownership, ground-floor position in a 1959 block, and a Wola market with active new-build supply pressure — each independently erode both rent ceiling and resale optionality. Investors with a sub-5% net yield threshold or a strong preference for full-ownership/standard-mortgage properties should pass at this price. **At approx. 1.15M PLN** (where gross yield clears 6.0% and net yield approaches 5.1%), the risk-adjusted case for acquisition becomes materially stronger and the verdict would shift to Buy.
