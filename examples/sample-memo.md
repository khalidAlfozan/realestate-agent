# Investment Memo: duże, widne mieszkanie Erazma z Zakroczymia

**Source:** https://www.otodom.pl/pl/oferta/duze-widne-mieszkanie-erazma-z-zakroczymia-ID4BlUH
**Asking price:** 1 530 000 PLN
**Surface:** 128.0 m² · 4 rooms
**Location:** ul. Myśliborska, Tarchomin, Białołęka, Warszawa

## 1. Property summary

This is a large, ground-floor 4-room apartment (128 m²) in a 10-storey concrete panel block (wielka płyta) situated in the gated Tarchomin estate in Białołęka. The building is listed with a 1989 build year, though the seller's description specifies 1993 — the discrepancy is minor and consistent with late-PRL/early-transition construction. The unit has full ownership (pełna własność) with a clean land-and-mortgage register, district (urban) heating — predictable and cheap — and is available immediately. Distinctive features include a private entrance via a west-facing garden, two balconies, three bathrooms, oak herringbone parquet, and claimed Carrara marble tiling; the layout is described as adaptable for small commercial use (medical, office, kindergarten). One parking space in the fenced residents' lot is included in the asking price.

## 2. Neighbourhood context

**District character.** Białołęka is Warsaw's northernmost, predominantly residential district — a low-rise and mid-rise suburban expanse popular with families seeking affordable space. The Tarchomin sub-neighbourhood, where this apartment sits, is a well-established 1990s estate on the right bank of the Vistula, accessible to the city centre via the Maria Skłodowska-Curie Bridge. The immediate environment is quiet and green, with a sports centre (indoor pool, tennis courts, football pitch) adjacent to the building. Target tenant profile is family/dual-income households rather than young professionals or expats, given the distance from central Warsaw and the lack of metro access.

**Demographics.** Białołęka is Warsaw's fastest-growing district: 161,428 residents (2024, GUS BDL) across 73 km², yielding a relatively low density of 2,210 persons/km². Net migration was +1,007 in 2024 — a solid demand tailwind — but must be read alongside a supply-pressure metric that is the most concerning in Warsaw: 14.4 new dwellings completed per 1,000 residents (2025, GUS BDL). This is far above typical Warsaw district levels and signals sustained rental supply pressure that structurally limits rent growth. There are 190 businesses per 1,000 residents (2024), a modest ratio pointing to a bedroom-community profile with limited local employment.

**Amenities.** There is no metro within the search radius — a material negative for rental premiums in Warsaw. Tram access is reasonable: 8 tram stops within 800m, with the nearest (Mehoffera 06 and Tarchomin 05) at around 420m. Bus coverage is abundant — 55 stops within 800m, with the closest an unnamed stop just 289m away and the Erazma z Zakroczymia 02 stop at 341m. Schools are excellent for a family-let scenario: Szkoła Podstawowa nr 344 (the one named in the seller's description) is only 155m away (within the wider 1,200m school catchment), and 10 further schools are within that radius. The nearest green space, Park Ogrody Mehoffera, is 410m away. Transit adequacy is decent for Białołęka but does not compensate for metro absence.

## 3. Market backdrop

NBP's 2024-Q1 housing-market report recorded Warsaw primary-market transaction prices rising 6.4% quarter-on-quarter and 24.1% year-on-year in early 2024 — but these gains relate to the city's primary market, not the outer-district secondary market. The same report notes that Warsaw secondary-market transaction prices rose 5.2% q/q and 10.0% y/y in 2024 Q1 (subject to revision, as the report notes data from the RCiWN register underestimate these figures due to the limited number of more expensive transactions). This headline strength is concentrated in central locations and does not translate uniformly to Białołęka.

On the rental side, NBP's 2023-Q4 and 2024-Q1 reports document an important cycle reversal: the number of dwellings offered for rent was still rising through 2023 as completions from the 2020–2023 investment boom entered the market, and transactional rental rates fell quarter-on-quarter in all city groups in 2024 Q1, reducing rental profitability (NBP-quarterly-2024-Q1). This is directly relevant here: Białołęka's 14.4 new completions per 1,000 residents is among the highest supply-pressure readings in Warsaw, and it is precisely the type of outer-ring, investor-heavy pipeline that the NBP reports identify as a source of rent compression.

Structurally, NBP analysis highlights that rental demand in Warsaw is sustained by in-migration, creditworthiness constraints preventing ownership, and foreign workers (historically from Ukraine). These factors support a baseline tenant pool, but they also disproportionately favour central or transit-connected locations. For an outer district without metro access, rising supply competes directly for a price-sensitive tenant segment, keeping rent ceilings low.

## 4. Condition assessment

The seller describes the apartment as well-maintained with original Carrara marble in the bathrooms and kitchen, a perfectly preserved oak parquet floor, fully replaced building installations, and a thermally insulated facade. The photo analysis returns **FAIR overall condition (high confidence, 12 photos analysed)**, which significantly contradicts these claims. While the herringbone oak parquet is present and the marble tiling is confirmed, both show substantial wear, staining, and discolouration inconsistent with "well-maintained" status. Kitchen cabinetry is 1990s-vintage with no modern appliances visible; bathroom fixtures appear original to the early-1990s build, showing corrosion on shower frames and grout discolouration. The garden is visibly overgrown with dead vegetation and moss-covered paving. Critically, no photographic evidence supports the claimed building-wide installation replacement despite seller assertions. Windows appear recently replaced, which is consistent with the facade insulation work, but this is the only material upgrade the photos corroborate. A buyer should budget for a meaningful kitchen and bathroom refurbishment before letting.

## 6. Financial analysis

- Asking price: **1 530 000 PLN** (= 11,953 PLN/m² vs sale comp median of 10,423 PLN/m² → **14.7% premium**).
- Monthly community fee (czynsz): **1 500 PLN** (≈ 11.72 PLN/m² — within the 8–15 PLN/m² normal range, but a heavy absolute burden on a 128 m² outer-district flat).
- Estimated monthly rent: **6 600 PLN**.
- Annual rent: **79 200 PLN**.
- **Gross yield: 5.18%** (from `calculate_gross_yield`).
- **Net rent after czynsz**: 6 600 - 1 500 = **5 100 PLN/month**; net yield ≈ (5 100 × 12) / 1 530 000 = **4.0%**.
- The gross yield of 5.18% is at the very bottom of Warsaw's typical 5–7% range; net yield of 4.0% is firmly below it. For an outer-district, no-metro, concrete-panel, ground-floor asset in fair condition, this yield level does not provide adequate compensation for illiquidity, renovation cost, and vacancy risk. The 1,500 PLN czynsz alone consumes 22.7% of gross rent, making net economics structurally weak even before vacancy, tax, or capex assumptions.

## 7. Risks and sensitivities

- **Supply compression.** Białołęka's 14.4 new completions per 1,000 residents (GUS BDL 2025) is the highest supply-pressure reading in Warsaw. NBP's 2024-Q1 report already documents falling transactional rental rates in the first quarter of 2024. A landlord in Tarchomin faces a growing pipeline of newer alternatives competing for a relatively price-sensitive family tenant base.
- **No metro access.** The absence of metro within the 800m radius is the single largest structural drag on achievable rent. All nearby transit is bus or tram; central Warsaw commutes run 40–60 minutes. This limits the tenant pool to car-owning families or those priced out of better-connected districts.
- **Condition and renovation capex.** Photos reveal FAIR condition with 1990s-era bathrooms, kitchen, and fittings that contradict seller claims. A buyer should budget at minimum 80,000–120,000 PLN for kitchen and bathroom refurbishment before the unit commands the rent modelled here; capitalised into yield, this further dilutes returns.
- **Ground-floor discount and security perception.** Ground-floor units are structurally harder to let and achieve lower rents. The private entrance — framed by the seller as a premium — may also raise security concerns for prospective tenants.
- **Price premium risk.** The 14.7% premium over comp-set median is the most actionable flag: if the seller holds to asking price and a buyer does not negotiate, the entry basis is misaligned with the local market for comparable assets. As noted in §5, the comp set median for this size/district is 10,423 PLN/m², implying a fair-value price closer to 1,334,000 PLN.
- **Czynsz drag.** At 1,500 PLN/month, the community fee consumes nearly 23% of gross rent. Any vacancy period (even 1 month/year) directly compounds this drag with no offset.
- **Potential commercial use risk.** The seller's marketing of the apartment as adaptable for office/medical/preschool use introduces ambiguity: if a buyer relies on commercial letting, they may face HOA or zoning restrictions and different tenant-law exposure. Residential rental investment assumptions require residential letting.

## 8. Recommendation

**Verdict:** Walk at asking price; Negotiate down.
**Confidence:** High.

The fundamental problem is a trifecta: a 14.7% price premium over comp-set median applied to a ground-floor, FAIR-condition, 1990s concrete-panel block in a no-metro outer district, yielding only 5.18% gross and 4.0% net after the 1,500 PLN monthly czynsz — inadequate for the risk profile. The photo analysis specifically and credibly contradicts the seller's renovation narrative, removing the only plausible justification for a premium price. **Walk at 1,530,000 PLN; would reconsider at approximately 1,260,000–1,330,000 PLN**, where gross yield clears 6.0–6.3% and the asking PLN/m² aligns with the comp-set median of 10,423 PLN/m². Even then, a buyer should price in 80,000–120,000 PLN of immediate refurbishment capex before the unit is lettable at the modelled rent. This is a v3 analysis with both rent and sale comparables, photo-based condition review (12 photos, high confidence), and NBP market-report context (quarterly reports 2023-Q3 through 2024-Q1).
